## §2.0 Lead-in (~140 words, procedural enumeration only)

This study integrates four complementary computational approaches to characterize 
the corpus of literature on traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) herbal-drug 
interactions published between 2005 and 2026. A search across Web of Science 
Core Collection, Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed was assembled into a single 
integrated corpus through a five-stage deduplication and precision-filter 
pipeline (Figure 1; Section 2.1-2.2), yielding 9,413 main-period records 
(2005-2025) and a 304-record partial 2026 extension. We then applied 
SPECTER2-embedding topic modeling (Section 2.3), large-language-model-based 
mechanism extraction under a fixed 16-category schema (Section 2.4), 
and two families of subsequent analyses: bibliometric convention and 
citation-burst analysis on the corpus as a whole (Section 2.5), and 
Fisher-exact enrichment testing across topic x mechanism x era and 
herbal-taxonomy tiers (Section 2.6).

## §2.1 Data sources and search strategy

We searched four bibliographic databases—Web of Science Core Collection (WoS), Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed—covering publications dated 1 January 2005 through 14 May 2026 (the date of search execution). The four-database design reflects three complementary objectives: maximizing coverage of biomedical and pharmacology journals via curated subscription services (WoS, Scopus), capturing open-metadata reach into traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) regional and Chinese-language sources via OpenAlex, and ensuring complete coverage of clinical and pharmacological case reports indexed by the National Library of Medicine via PubMed. WoS and Scopus were used in combination rather than substitution because their journal-coverage and citation-indexing scopes are known to differ for non-English regional pharmacology literature; OpenAlex provided open reach into sources underindexed by subscription databases; PubMed contributed the MeSH controlled vocabulary for biomedical concept matching.

Search strings followed a two-axis Boolean structure: a TCM-term axis intersected with a herb–drug interaction axis. The TCM axis combined free-text terms ("traditional Chinese medicine", "Chinese herbal medicine", "Chinese materia medica", "TCM herb", "Chinese formula"), botanical Linnaean genera prominently used in TCM (e.g. *Hypericum*, *Ginkgo*, *Panax*, *Astragalus*, *Glycyrrhiza*, *Salvia*, *Curcuma*), and named TCM formula categories. The interaction axis combined drug-interaction predicates ("drug interaction", "herb–drug interaction", "pharmacokinetic interaction", "pharmacodynamic interaction"), drug-metabolizing and transporter targets relevant to herbal pharmacology (CYP1A2, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, CYP2E1, CYP3A4, UGT family, P-glycoprotein, PXR, BCRP), and bioavailability and absorption-modulation vocabulary. Database-specific syntax was implemented per each platform's controlled-vocabulary conventions: WoS used Topic (TS=) field searches across title, abstract, author keywords, and KeywordPlus; Scopus used TITLE-ABS-KEY field searches; OpenAlex used full-text-indexed metadata with concept tagging; PubMed used MeSH-augmented combinations with explosion. Full Boolean strings for each database, including all controlled-vocabulary terms and field qualifiers, are provided in Supplementary Methods S1.

The 1 January 2005 lower bound was chosen to provide a 21-year complete-year retrospective window spanning the modern indexed-publication era for TCM herbal–drug interaction research; this period encompasses the post-millennium scientific response to the prominent St. John's wort–warfarin and St. John's wort–CYP3A4 case-report literature of the early 2000s (Section 3.2, Era 1). Records dated 1 January 2005 through 31 December 2025 were retained as the main 21-year complete-year corpus; records dated in 2026 were retained as a partial-year extension because 2026 indexing was incomplete at search execution. The four databases together returned 16,652 records prior to deduplication and corpus integration (Section 2.2).

## §2.2 Corpus integration and deduplication

Records from the four databases were integrated into a single corpus through a five-stage deduplication and precision-filtering pipeline summarized in Figure 1, with stage-wise record counts of 16,652 → 9,802 → 9,536 → 9,438 → 9,413 (main 2005–2025 corpus) and a parallel 304-record 2026 partial extension. The pipeline was deliberately ordered so that high-confidence identifier-based deduplication (Stages 1–2) preceded text-similarity deduplication (Stage 3), which in turn preceded the precision filter applied specifically to OpenAlex (Stage 4) and the cross-file reconciliation between the main and 2026-partial subcorpora (Stage 5).

Stage 1 imported per-database raw exports and normalized field schemas. Stage 2 deduplicated on the digital object identifier (DOI) as a primary key, with DOIs lowercased and `https://doi.org/` URL prefixes stripped to canonicalize comparison. Records without a DOI were carried forward to subsequent stages and matched on textual fields. Stage 3 applied fuzzy title-based deduplication using token-level Levenshtein similarity with a similarity ratio threshold of ≥ 95, blocked on publication year and first-author surname to constrain the comparison space; this follows the default tolerance (`tol = 0.95`) implemented in the bibliometrix R package and substantially reduces the false-merge risk associated with naive all-pairs comparison.

Stage 4 implemented an OpenAlex-specific three-block precision filter to address the higher false-positive rate observed in OpenAlex retrievals during pilot inspection. OpenAlex records were classified into four blocks based on title and abstract content: B1 records matched the conjunction of TCM, drug, and interaction terms; B2 records matched TCM and interaction terms but not drug terms; B3 records matched TCM and CYP/transporter terms but no explicit interaction predicate; and B0 records matched none of the three patterns. Records classified as B1, B2, or B3 were retained as TCM-relevant; B0 records (98 records) were dropped from the OpenAlex contribution. The retained OpenAlex contributions entered the integrated corpus alongside WoS, Scopus, and PubMed records.

Stage 5 performed a cross-file reconciliation between the main 2005–2025 corpus and the 2026 partial-year extension to resolve records that had been independently retrieved into both subcorpora. Twenty-five main-corpus records dated 2026 were reassigned or dropped at this stage (24 dropped as duplicates of partial-corpus entries, 1 reassigned to the partial subcorpus), and 13 partial-corpus records were dropped due to DOI overlap with main-corpus entries. The final integrated corpus consisted of 9,413 main records (2005–2025) and 304 partial 2026 records (9,717 records in total). Field coverage in the final corpus was 87.1% for DOI, 97.0% for abstract (≥ 50 characters), and 100.0% for publication year. Records appeared in 1 (61.4%), 2 (16.6%), 3 (10.1%), or 4 (11.9%) of the four databases prior to integration, supporting the use of multi-database retrieval as a coverage-maximization strategy.

## §2.3 Topic modeling

Topic modeling was performed on the integrated corpus through a three-stage embedding-and-clustering pipeline: dense semantic embedding with SPECTER2, non-linear dimensionality reduction with UMAP, and density-based clustering with HDBSCAN. This pipeline replaces classical bag-of-words topic models (LDA, NMF) with embedding-space clustering on the rationale that scientific abstracts use specialized terminology with high lexical variation but compact semantic signature, which dense pre-trained scientific-paper encoders capture more efficiently than sparse-token methods.

Document embeddings were computed using SPECTER2 (`allenai/specter2`), a Transformer-based encoder pre-trained on scientific paper citation contexts to produce a 768-dimensional embedding per (title, abstract) pair. SPECTER2 was selected over general-purpose sentence encoders because its pre-training objective explicitly optimizes scientific-document similarity rather than generic semantic similarity. Title and abstract were concatenated with a separator token before encoding, following the SPECTER2 reference protocol. Embeddings for all records were computed once and cached to disk as a record-keyed parquet file to permit deterministic re-runs of downstream clustering without recomputing the embedding stage.

The 768-dimensional embedding space was projected to a 15-dimensional manifold using Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) with the following parameters: `n_neighbors = 30`, `min_dist = 0.0`, and `metric = cosine`. These settings prioritize the preservation of local neighbourhood structure—appropriate for downstream density-based clustering—over global geometric fidelity. Clustering was then performed on the 15-dimensional UMAP space using Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN) with `min_cluster_size = 80`, `min_samples = 10`, and `cluster_selection_method = "eom"`. The 80-record minimum cluster size was selected to produce thematically interpretable clusters at the scale of the integrated corpus; smaller thresholds produced an unstable number of micro-clusters in pilot runs, and larger thresholds collapsed distinct thematic subareas into composite groups.

Cluster labels were generated by extracting the top n-gram terms per cluster using KeyBERT and refining the candidate term list through a large-language-model-based decontamination step (gpt-4o-mini, OpenAI), in which the model was given five randomly sampled abstracts from each cluster together with a list of candidate terms and asked to remove terms that appeared frequently across multiple clusters (for example generic methodology vocabulary such as "study", "method", "analysis"). The decontamination prompt was applied in three iterative passes per cluster to converge on a stable, cluster-distinctive label. Final cluster labels in Sections 3 and 4 are reported as space-separated top-three term combinations (for example "cyp inhibition / cyp enzymes / cyp isoforms" for cluster #30).

HDBSCAN produced 39 thematic clusters (cluster ids 0 through 38) and assigned 3,437 records (approximately 36% of the corpus) to the noise cluster (cluster_id = -1). The noise assignment is an intrinsic feature of density-based clustering and identifies records whose semantic position is not sufficiently close to any dense neighbourhood; it is not a clustering failure and is retained as a corpus property. Downstream analyses (Sections 2.5 and 2.6) operated on the 39 thematic clusters and treated the -1 noise as a separate analytical category, with the Fisher enrichment analysis additionally restricting to the 37 clusters with sufficient mechanism-extraction support (Section 2.6).

## §2.4 LLM-based mechanism extraction

Pharmacological mechanism information was extracted from each abstract using a controlled large-language-model (LLM) pipeline that returned structured JSON conforming to a fixed schema rather than free-text mechanism descriptions. Downstream statistical enrichment analyses (Sections 2.6 and 3.4) require categorical mechanism labels rather than open-vocabulary mentions, and the structured-extraction approach also permits direct benchmarking against a human-coded reference set at the categorical level.

The extraction schema (Schema v3) defines 16 mechanism categories representing the major classes of herbal–drug interaction in the pharmacological literature. The categories partition into four families: pharmacokinetic (for example CYP_inhibition, CYP_induction, UGT_inhibition, P-glycoprotein_inhibition, transporter_modulation, absorption_alteration), pharmacodynamic (for example receptor_agonism, receptor_antagonism, receptor_synergism, synergistic_efficacy, signaling_pathway_modulation), toxicological (additive_toxicity, organ_toxicity_modulation), and two catch-all categories ("unspecified", "other") used when no specific mechanism could be identified or when the mentioned mechanism fell outside the controlled vocabulary. Each extracted interaction was returned as a JSON object containing herb identifier(s), drug or drug-class identifier(s), the mechanism category, and an extraction-confidence score on a [0, 1] scale. The schema required identifier and category fields to validate against pre-specified enumerations during JSON parsing; malformed responses were returned to the model for one retry before being flagged as extraction failures.

Extraction was performed using the OpenAI `gpt-4o-mini` model accessed via the OpenRouter API gateway. This model was selected after pilot benchmarks for its favourable cost-to-categorical-accuracy ratio at the scale of the integrated corpus (approximately 9.7 thousand abstracts). The OpenRouter routing layer provided automatic failover across multiple inference providers, retry-on-error behaviour, and request-level latency and provider-availability diagnostics, which improved end-to-end completion reliability over direct provider calls during the pilot phase.

Prior to full-corpus extraction, a stratified pilot benchmark of n = 200 records was extracted and human-coded for categorical mechanism agreement. The stratification was on three axes—publication year (early, middle, recent thirds of the window), assigned HDBSCAN topic cluster, and journal impact tier—to sample the diversity of writing style and mechanism reporting present in the full corpus. Disagreement cases between LLM extraction and human coding were used to refine the system prompt and to add normalization rules for known target-vocabulary variants (for example, "CYP3A4 induction" and "induction of CYP3A4" both mapping to CYP_induction); records originally labelled "other" by the pilot extraction were re-extracted with an extended prompt clarifying the catch-all boundary, yielding a small downward correction to the final "other" fraction.

Full-corpus extraction was applied to all 9,717 integrated records, with raw model responses stored as record-keyed parquet files to allow deterministic re-parsing and re-filtering without re-querying the model. The extraction returned at least one parsable interaction record for approximately 3,100 abstracts (approximately 33% of the corpus); the remainder returned an explicit "no extractable interaction" annotation, indicating that the abstract did not contain a structured herbal–drug interaction claim. For downstream enrichment analyses (Section 2.6), interactions were retained only if their extraction-confidence score was ≥ 0.7 and their mechanism category was not in the {"unspecified", "other"} set, yielding 1,662 records with at least one high-confidence mechanism assignment and 1,738 (record, mechanism) pairs.

## §2.5 Bibliometric and citation-burst analyses

Two classical bibliometric tool families were applied to the corpus to characterize the field's structure: bibliometric convention analysis using the bibliometrix R package (Aria & Cuccurullo, 2017, *Journal of Informetrics* 11(4):959–975), and citation-burst analysis using CiteSpace version 6.4.R1 (Chen, 2006, *Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology* 57(3):359–377). Both tools were applied to a WoS-only subset of the integrated corpus (n = 3,091 of 9,413 main records, 32.7%) rather than to the full integrated corpus, because both tools require WoS-format input fields—affiliation strings (C1), full-document citation counts (TC), and the separation of author keywords (DE) from KeywordPlus (ID)—that could not be reconstructed with the same standardization confidence after cross-database harmonization. The WoS subset preserves the citation graph required by both methods at the cost of some coverage; this trade-off is quantified in Section 3.2 by comparing the average citations per document in the WoS subset with the integrated corpus. All other analyses, including topic modeling (Section 2.3), mechanism extraction (Section 2.4), and statistical enrichment (Section 2.6), operated on the full integrated 9,413 main-corpus record set.

The bibliometric convention analyses produced annual publication production curves, Lotka's-law author productivity distributions (slope α and R² goodness-of-fit), Bradford's-law journal core-zone partitioning (multiplier and zone counts), the thematic map (centrality × density quadrant plot of keyword clusters), and the three-fields plot (authors–keywords–journals correspondence), using the bibliometrix package's default parameter values throughout. Citation-burst detection used Kleinberg's burst algorithm as implemented in CiteSpace, with the following parameter settings: 22 one-year time slices spanning 2005–2026, g-index node selection (k = 25), cosine link strength, Pathfinder network pruning, modularity-based cluster detection, log-likelihood-ratio (LLR) label extraction, and burst parameters γ = 0.5 with a minimum burst duration of 2 years. The γ = 0.5 setting (rather than the more conservative default γ = 1.0) was selected because pilot analyses showed that γ = 1.0 produced very few bursts in this corpus, missing several keywords that visibly underwent rise-and-fall dynamics in the annual production data. A cross-tool consistency check was performed between the bibliometrix three-fields plot and the CiteSpace burst-keyword set; the two methods independently identified the same set of historically prominent keywords for the 2005–2012 era—St. John's wort, ginkgo biloba, milk thistle, and grapefruit juice—supporting the analytical robustness of each.

## §2.6 Statistical enrichment analyses

The mechanism-level statistical analyses rest on a workhorse procedure—Fisher's exact test applied independently to each cell of a contingency table, followed by Benjamini–Hochberg false-discovery-rate (BH-FDR) correction—applied across three orthogonal stratification axes: topic × mechanism (static), topic × mechanism × era (temporal), and herb-taxonomy tier × mechanism (cross-resolution). Together with the bibliometric and citation-burst analyses of Section 2.5, these three stratifications form the four-method triangulation framework reported in Section 3.3 and developed throughout Section 3. All statistical analyses operated on the integrated 9,413 main-corpus record set rather than the WoS subset of Section 2.5, because enrichment tests depend on topic and mechanism labels that were available across all four databases.

The static topic-by-mechanism analysis was performed on a 37 × 16 contingency matrix derived from N = 1,738 (record, mechanism) pairs spanning 1,662 unique records (two HDBSCAN topics were excluded for insufficient mechanism-extraction support). Each of the 592 cells was tested with a two-sided Fisher exact test on the 2 × 2 (in-topic vs. not-in-topic) × (has-mechanism vs. no-mechanism) tabulation, with Benjamini–Hochberg false-discovery-rate correction applied across all 592 simultaneous tests. The cell-level significance threshold was q < 0.05; strong enrichment additionally required odds ratio > 2 and at least five observed in-topic-with-mechanism records, and strong depletion required odds ratio < 0.5 and at least five expected in-topic-with-mechanism records. For the heatmap visualization (Figure 8), the log2 odds ratio was computed using a Haldane–Anscombe correction (adding 0.5 to all four cells of the 2 × 2 table) to avoid undefined values when cells contained zero observations; this correction was applied only to the visualization and was not used to compute Fisher p-values, which remained based on the exact hypergeometric distribution.

For the era-stratified analysis, the same 1,738 pairs were partitioned into three disjoint publication eras (Scheme B): P1 (2005–2013, n = 517 pairs), P2 (2014–2019, n = 687), and P3 (2020–2026, n = 534). Era boundaries were chosen by two simultaneous criteria: alignment with the CiteSpace burst-era structure reported in Section 3.2, and approximate balance in subsample size across eras. The Fisher exact test was re-run per (topic, mechanism, era) cell, producing a per-era q-value. Across the 40 cells that reached significance in at least one era, an eight-class trajectory typology was applied: STABLE (significant in all three eras), FADING (P1 only), TRANSIENT (P2 only), EMERGING (P3 only), DECLINING (monotonically decreasing observed count across eras), RISING (monotonically increasing), BIMODAL (P1 and P3 but not P2), and WEAK_NONE (significant in at least one era but with no consistent across-era pattern).

For the three-tier herbal-taxonomy analysis, the herb-taxonomy fields extracted by Schema v3 (Section 2.4) were used to construct three separate contingency matrices at the botanical-family, species, and chemical-compound tiers. Each tier applied a minimum-occurrence threshold to ensure non-trivial cell counts: 24 families meeting n ≥ 10, 32 species meeting n ≥ 10, and 23 compounds meeting n ≥ 5. The lower threshold at the compound tier was justified by the heterogeneous per-compound record distribution (median of approximately 1.8 records per compound) and by the methodological observation that single-compound mechanistic studies do not aggregate per-compound the way per-species or per-family studies do. All three tiers were restricted to the same 1,676-record pool with at least one mapped taxonomic identifier (the in_map = True restriction), so that resolution-tier comparisons across the same chain of identifiers (family → species → compound) reflected biological resolution rather than coverage-driven artefacts. Fisher exact tests with BH-FDR correction were performed across all 1,241 cells (384 + 512 + 345) of the three matrices simultaneously, producing a single corpus-wide q-value distribution comparable across tiers. For each herb chain (family → species → compound for the same herb), an eight-class chain typology classified the cross-tier resolution pattern, including FULL_CHAIN (significant at all three tiers for the same mechanism), FAMILY_PERVASIVE (significant only at family), SPECIES_SPECIFIC (significant only at species), and additional composite classes for partial chains.

Across all analyses, findings significant in two or more methods (bibliometric, citation-burst, static Fisher, era-stratified Fisher) were reported as multi-method-anchored and are summarized in Table 1 (Section 3.3); cases significant in one method only are explicitly flagged as method-specific. This convention provides a consistent reporting standard for the trade-off between statistical sensitivity (more findings) and methodological robustness (more durable findings).

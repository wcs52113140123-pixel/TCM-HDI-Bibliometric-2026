## §2.0 Lead-in

This study integrates four complementary computational approaches to characterize the TCM herbal–drug interaction literature published 2005–2026. A search across Web of Science Core Collection, Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed was assembled into a single integrated corpus through a five-stage deduplication and precision-filter pipeline (Figure 1; §2.1–2.2), yielding 9,413 main-period records (2005–2025) and a 304-record partial 2026 extension. We then applied SPECTER2-embedding topic modeling (§2.3); large-language-model-based mechanism extraction under a fixed 16-category schema (§2.4); bibliometric convention and citation-burst analyses (§2.5); and Fisher-exact enrichment testing across topic × mechanism × era and herbal-taxonomy strata (§2.6).

---

## §2.1 Data sources and search strategy

We searched four bibliographic databases—Web of Science Core Collection (WoS), Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed—covering 1 January 2005 through 14 May 2026 (search execution date). The four-database design balances curated journal coverage (WoS, Scopus), open-metadata reach into regional and Chinese-language TCM sources (OpenAlex), and complete NLM-indexed clinical and pharmacological case reports (PubMed). Search strings followed a two-axis Boolean structure: a TCM-term axis (free-text TCM terms, prominent Linnaean genera prominently used in TCM such as *Hypericum*, *Ginkgo*, *Panax*, *Astragalus*, *Glycyrrhiza*, *Salvia*, *Curcuma*, and named formula categories) intersected with a herb–drug interaction axis (interaction predicates; drug-metabolizing and transporter targets including CYP1A2/2C9/2C19/2D6/2E1/3A4, UGT family, P-glycoprotein, PXR, BCRP; bioavailability and absorption-modulation vocabulary). Database-specific syntax followed each platform's conventions (Topic for WoS, TITLE-ABS-KEY for Scopus, full-text-indexed metadata for OpenAlex, MeSH-augmented combinations with explosion for PubMed). Full Boolean strings, including all controlled-vocabulary terms and field qualifiers, are in Supplementary Methods S1.1. The 1 January 2005 lower bound provides a 21-year complete-year retrospective window, encompassing the post-millennium scientific response to the prominent St. John's wort–warfarin and St. John's wort–CYP3A4 case-report literature of the early 2000s (§3.2, Era 1). The four databases together returned 16,652 records prior to deduplication.

## §2.2 Corpus integration and deduplication

Records from the four databases were integrated through a five-stage deduplication and precision-filtering pipeline (Figure 1; full per-stage rules in Supplementary Methods S1.2), with stage-wise counts 16,652 → 9,802 → 9,536 → 9,438 → 9,413 (main 2005–2025) and a parallel 304-record 2026 partial extension. Stages 1–2 imported per-database raw exports, normalized field schemas, and deduplicated on DOI (lowercased, URL-prefix-stripped) as primary key. Stage 3 applied fuzzy title-based deduplication on non-DOI records (token-level Levenshtein similarity ratio ≥ 0.95, blocked on publication year and first-author surname; following the bibliometrix R-package default tolerance). Stage 4 applied an OpenAlex-specific precision filter classifying records into four content blocks (TCM × drug × interaction; TCM × interaction; TCM × CYP/transporter; none); 98 unrelated records were dropped. Stage 5 reconciled the main and 2026-partial subcorpora (24 dropped, 1 reassigned, 13 partial-corpus dropped). The final integrated corpus comprised 9,413 main records and 304 partial 2026 records (9,717 total). Field coverage: 87.1% DOI, 97.0% abstract (≥ 50 characters), 100% publication year. Records appeared in 1 (61.4%), 2 (16.6%), 3 (10.1%), or 4 (11.9%) of the four databases prior to integration, supporting multi-database retrieval as a coverage-maximization strategy.

## §2.3 Topic modeling

Topic modeling used a three-stage embedding-and-clustering pipeline (full parameter justification in Supplementary Methods S1.3): SPECTER2 document embedding, UMAP non-linear dimensionality reduction, and HDBSCAN density-based clustering, replacing classical bag-of-words methods (LDA, NMF) with embedding-space clustering. Document embeddings used SPECTER2 (`allenai/specter2`) on concatenated title + abstract pairs, producing 768-dimensional vectors per record (cached for deterministic re-runs). UMAP projected these to 15 dimensions (`n_neighbors = 30`, `min_dist = 0.0`, cosine metric), prioritizing local neighbourhood structure for downstream density-based clustering. HDBSCAN with `min_cluster_size = 80`, `min_samples = 10`, and `cluster_selection_method = "eom"` produced 39 thematic clusters (ids 0–38) and assigned 3,437 records (~36% of corpus) to noise (-1), which is an intrinsic feature of density-based clustering rather than a failure. Cluster labels were generated through KeyBERT top-n-gram extraction refined by three iterative LLM-based decontamination passes (gpt-4o-mini, OpenAI) that removed cross-cluster generic vocabulary (e.g. "study", "method", "analysis"). Downstream analyses operated on the 39 thematic clusters, with the Fisher enrichment analysis additionally restricting to 37 clusters with sufficient mechanism-extraction support (§2.6).

## §2.4 LLM-based mechanism extraction

Pharmacological mechanism information was extracted from each abstract using a controlled LLM pipeline returning structured JSON conforming to a fixed schema, supporting categorical enrichment analyses (§2.6, §3.4) and direct benchmarking against human-coded references. Schema v3 defines 16 mechanism categories in four families: pharmacokinetic (CYP_inhibition, CYP_induction, UGT_inhibition, P-glycoprotein_inhibition, transporter_modulation, absorption_alteration), pharmacodynamic (receptor_agonism, receptor_antagonism, receptor_synergism, synergistic_efficacy, signaling_pathway_modulation), toxicological (additive_toxicity, organ_toxicity_modulation), and two catch-alls ("unspecified", "other"). Each extracted interaction returned JSON with herb identifier(s), drug or drug-class identifier(s), mechanism category, and an extraction-confidence score on [0, 1]; identifier and category fields validated against pre-specified enumerations during JSON parsing, with malformed responses retried once before being flagged. Full prompt text and per-category definitions are in Supplementary Methods S2.

Extraction used OpenAI's `gpt-4o-mini` via the OpenRouter API gateway, selected for its cost-to-categorical-accuracy ratio at the scale of ~9.7k abstracts. The routing layer provided automatic failover, retry-on-error behaviour, and request-level diagnostics. Full-corpus extraction returned at least one parsable interaction for ~3,100 of 9,717 abstracts (~33%); the remainder returned an explicit "no extractable interaction" annotation. For enrichment analyses (§2.6), interactions were retained at extraction-confidence ≥ 0.7 with mechanism category not in {"unspecified", "other"}, yielding 1,662 records with high-confidence assignments and 1,738 (record, mechanism) pairs.

LLM zero-shot extraction was chosen over manual curation (does not scale to corpus size and limits external reproducibility) and keyword-regex matching against curated vocabularies (cannot resolve negation, scope, or context-dependent qualifiers routine in pharmacological abstracts); BERT-family domain-specific models (BioBERT, ClinicalBERT) would require task-specific fine-tuning corpora not available for the 16-category Schema v3 taxonomy. The trade-off—per-record accuracy cost characterised in the independent-annotator validation reported in Supplementary Methods S2 (Cohen's κ = 0.612 overall; κ = 0.867 in the high-confidence stratum; 78.7% relaxed agreement)—is acceptable for full corpus coverage and direct portability across schemas. The trade-off is discussed in §5.

## §2.5 Bibliometric and citation-burst analyses

Classical bibliometric analysis used the bibliometrix R package (Aria & Cuccurullo, 2017, *Journal of Informetrics* 11(4):959–975) for thematic-map and three-fields-plot construction, and CiteSpace v6.4.R1 (Chen, 2006, *JASIST* 57(3):359–377) for citation-burst detection. Both tools were applied to a WoS-only subset (n = 3,091 of 9,413 main records; 32.7%) rather than the full integrated corpus because both require WoS-format fields (C1 affiliations, TC citation counts, separation of author keywords DE from KeywordPlus ID) that could not be reconstructed with comparable confidence after cross-database harmonization. The WoS subset preserves the citation graph required by both methods at the cost of some coverage; all other analyses (§2.3, §2.4, §2.6) operated on the full integrated 9,413 main-corpus record set. CiteSpace burst detection used Kleinberg's algorithm with γ = 0.5 (selected over the more conservative default γ = 1.0 because pilot inspection showed γ = 1.0 produced very few bursts in this corpus, missing keywords with visible rise-and-fall dynamics) and minimum duration 2 years over 22 one-year time slices. Full bibliometric and burst configurations are in Supplementary Methods S1.4.

## §2.6 Statistical enrichment analyses

Mechanism-level statistical analyses applied Fisher's exact test cell-wise to contingency tables with Benjamini–Hochberg false-discovery-rate (BH-FDR) correction, across three orthogonal stratifications: topic × mechanism (static), topic × mechanism × era (temporal), and herb-taxonomy tier × mechanism (cross-resolution). Together with bibliometric and citation-burst analyses (§2.5), these form the four-method triangulation framework reported in §3.3. Analyses operated on the integrated 9,413 main-corpus record set; full statistical methodology including contingency-table construction, BH-FDR implementation, trajectory/chain typology rules, and multiple-testing burden analysis is in Supplementary Methods S3.

The static topic × mechanism analysis used a 37 × 16 contingency matrix from N = 1,738 (record, mechanism) pairs spanning 1,662 unique records (two topics excluded for insufficient mechanism support). All 592 cells were tested with two-sided Fisher exact tests with BH-FDR correction at threshold q < 0.05; strong enrichment additionally required odds ratio > 2 and ≥ 5 observed records, strong depletion OR < 0.5 and ≥ 5 expected records.

Era-stratification partitioned the 1,738 pairs into three publication eras—P1 (2005–2013, n = 517), P2 (2014–2019, n = 687), P3 (2020–2026, n = 534)—aligned to the CiteSpace burst-era structure (§3.2) with balanced subsample sizes. Across the 40 cells reaching significance in at least one era, an 8-class trajectory typology classified the temporal pattern (STABLE, FADING, TRANSIENT, EMERGING, DECLINING, RISING, BIMODAL, WEAK_NONE).

Three-tier herbal-taxonomy analysis constructed separate family, species, and compound contingency matrices (24 families with n ≥ 10; 32 species with n ≥ 10; 23 compounds with n ≥ 5), all restricted to the same 1,676-record pool with at least one mapped taxonomic identifier so that resolution-tier comparisons reflect biological resolution rather than coverage. Fisher tests with BH-FDR across all 1,241 cells (384 + 512 + 345) produced a corpus-wide q-value distribution comparable across tiers; per-herb chain typology classified cross-tier patterns (FULL_CHAIN, FAMILY_PERVASIVE, SPECIES_SPECIFIC, COMPOUND_ONLY, plus four composite classes).

## §4.1 The PK–PD structural bifurcation

The most consequential structural finding is that the TCM herbal–drug interaction literature bifurcates into two statistically non-overlapping research spaces (Section 3.4). Pharmacokinetic-named topics and indication-named topics populate disjoint regions of the topic–mechanism contingency space, with mutual exclusion reaching q = 10⁻⁴⁷ (topic #11 anticancer × CYP_inhibition: 5 observed against 99.7 expected). Era-stratification (Section 3.5) shows this is a 22-year structural invariant rather than recent drift: 18 of 40 trajectory-classifiable cells were STABLE across the three publication eras (P1 2005–2013, P2 2014–2019, P3 2020–2026). We interpret the bifurcation as reflecting the field's intellectual organisation: pharmacokinetic research matures around inducer or substrate compounds and converges on enzyme-class language, while pharmacodynamic research matures around therapeutic indications and converges on outcome language. A single paradigm case bridges both axes—the *Hypericum perforatum*–CYP3A4 induction axis, taken up in Section 4.2 as the principal four-method anchor of this study.

## §4.2 The St. John's wort–CYP3A4 paradigm as a four-method anchor

The SJW–CYP3A4 finding is recovered independently by all four signal sources of the framework: a Quadrant 2 niche-frontier position in the bibliometrix WoS thematic map (Section 3.1); the strongest citation burst in the corpus (CiteSpace burst strength 27.01, 2005–2012; Section 3.2); enrichment of HDBSCAN topic #5 ("antidepressants / wort sjw") for the CYP_induction mechanism category (OR = 26.7, q = 9.5 × 10⁻¹⁵; Section 3.4); and a FULL_CHAIN three-tier herbal signature with monotonically increasing odds ratios across resolution layers (Hypericaceae 14.20; *H. perforatum* 14.71; hyperforin 18.73; Section 3.6). The case is unusually clean: Hypericaceae is effectively monogeneric in the mechanistic literature, hyperforin is the dominant characterised constituent, and the SJW–PXR–CYP3A4 induction pathway is mechanistically resolved. We use this case as a calibration anchor against which other herbal–drug interaction patterns can be compared, not as a representative example.

## §4.3 Mechanism-specific natural resolution layers

Across the 13 cells reaching significance in the three-tier herbal analysis (Section 3.6), the resolution at which a mechanism produces a detectable signal varies systematically with the mechanism class. CYP_induction concentrates at the compound tier (hyperforin × CYP_induction is the only compound-tier hit). UGT_inhibition resolves at the species tier (*Glycyrrhiza uralensis* drives the species signal but is diluted at the Fabaceae family tier). Transporter modulation resolves at the family tier (Lamiaceae shows FAMILY_PERVASIVE enrichment with no single species individually carrying the signal). We interpret this as substantive biological-scale variation: enzyme induction depends on specific ligand–receptor geometry between inducer compounds and the PXR ligand-binding domain (compound); transporter modulation acts through structural classes—polyphenols, terpenes, lignans—distributed across botanical families (family); UGT-inhibitory chemotypes occupy an intermediate species-scoped scale (species). The discovery-rate gradient (family 25.0%, species 15.6%, compound 4.3%) thus reflects an intrinsic biological-scale axis of the literature, suggesting a resolution-aware framework in which the inferential target differs by mechanism class.

## §4.8 Synthesis: clinical implications and future directions

Three findings from this work carry implications beyond bibliometric description. The PK–PD bifurcation indicates the herbal–drug interaction literature has matured along two parallel research traditions whose mutual exclusion is structurally informative: clinical HDI risk stratification should mirror this axis, separately addressing pharmacokinetic perturbation (CYP3A4 induction by SJW-like inducers; P-glycoprotein modulation by structural classes) from pharmacodynamic interaction (anticancer–herb synergism; receptor-pathway interference). The mechanism-specific resolution layers further suggest the appropriate inferential target differs by mechanism class: compound-level identification for CYP induction; species-level chemotypic characterisation for UGT inhibition; family-level structural-class screening for transporter modulation. The framework itself is portable beyond TCM: any biomedical literature corpus with citation-graph and controlled-vocabulary annotation can be processed under the same convergence-anchored reporting convention. Future extensions include expansion to broader natural-product and dietary-supplement corpora; linkage of the bibliometric topic structure to electronic-health-record-derived interaction-event series, testing whether the literature-derived bifurcation predicts clinically observed adverse events; and predictive modelling using the topic–mechanism enrichment structure as a prior for new herb–drug pair risk classification.

Taken together, the four-method computational framework introduced here exhibits four properties that distinguish it from prior bibliometric-only or extraction-only approaches. First, triangulation across four methods with different failure modes—citation-graph community detection, Kleinberg-burst temporal analysis, FDR-corrected topic × mechanism enrichment, and three-tier taxonomic stratification—provides cross-method robustness; findings recovered by three or more methods (as is the case for *H. perforatum* × CYP3A4 induction) are markedly more confidence-bearing than single-method findings. Second, the explicit taxonomic-resolution layer transforms what would otherwise be an irreducible compound-vs-species-vs-family modelling choice into a discoverable property of the data, allowing the framework to identify the natural resolution at which different mechanism classes are best characterised. Third, temporal stratification distinguishes long-running structural invariants (such as the 22-year pharmacokinetic–pharmacodynamic bifurcation) from recent topical drift, an axis of analysis absent in static bibliometric snapshots. Fourth, the framework is portable: any biomedical literature corpus equipped with citation-graph and controlled-vocabulary annotation can be processed through the same analytical pipeline, supporting application to neighbouring domains including drug–drug interaction prediction, pharmacogenomic relation extraction, and adverse-event surveillance literature.

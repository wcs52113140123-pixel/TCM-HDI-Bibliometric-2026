## §4.0 Discussion lead-in

A methodological motif throughout this work is multi-method triangulation. Where a finding is independently supported by bibliometric convention analysis (the bibliometrix R package, applied to the WoS subset; Section 3.1), citation burst detection (CiteSpace, applied to the same subset; Section 3.2), structural cross-analysis (the topic × mechanism Fisher enrichment introduced here; Sections 3.4–3.5), and herbal-taxonomy stratification (the three-tier herb × mechanism analysis introduced here; Section 3.6), we treat that finding as robust to any single method's assumptions. Signals appearing in only one method's view are flagged for cautious interpretation rather than discarded. The strongest convergent example—*Hypericum perforatum* (St. John's wort) as a CYP3A4 inducer, recovered independently by all four methods (bibliometrix authorship-network Q2 frontier; CiteSpace Era 1 burst; Topic × Mechanism Fisher enrichment in topic #5; three-tier herbal FULL_CHAIN exemplar)—anchors the case-based discussion that follows.

## §4.1 The PK–PD structural bifurcation

The most consequential structural finding of this work is that the Chinese-medicine herbal–drug interaction literature is bifurcated into two statistically non-overlapping research spaces (Section 3.4). Topics named by a pharmacokinetic mechanism (#30 CYP inhibition, #24 UGT inhibition, #32 drug transporters, #9 PXR) and topics named by a clinical application (#11 anticancer, #23 antimicrobial, #28 medicinal plants / anxiolytic) populate two disjoint regions of the topic–mechanism contingency space, with mutual statistical exclusion reaching q = 10⁻⁴⁷ at the extreme (topic #11 × CYP_inhibition: 5 observed against 99.7 expected). This bifurcation appears as a clean cross-tabulation pattern with directly interpretable cell-level statistics, independent of whether the data are approached through the HDBSCAN topic structure or through the LLM-extracted mechanism schema.

The era-stratified analysis (Section 3.5) further refines this finding from a static observation into a temporal claim. Eighteen of the 40 trajectory-classifiable enrichment cells were classified STABLE—significant in all three publication eras P1 (2005–2013), P2 (2014–2019), and P3 (2020–2026)—and the strongest depletions, including topic #30 × synergistic_efficacy, retained zero observed records in every era. The PK–PD bifurcation is therefore not a recent fashion in TCM-HDI publishing but a foundational property of the field that has held for the entire 21-year window of indexed publication.

We interpret this bifurcation as a reflection of the field's intellectual organisation rather than an artefact of authorship vocabulary. Pharmacokinetic research lines mature around an inducer or substrate compound and converge on enzyme-class language; pharmacodynamic research lines mature around a disease context or therapeutic indication and converge on outcome language. The cross-tabulation contains a single paradigm case in which both traditions converge on the same molecular target—the *Hypericum perforatum*–CYP3A4 axis—which Section 4.2 takes up as the principal four-method anchor of this study.

## §4.2 The St. John's wort–CYP3A4 paradigm as a four-method anchor

The methodological strength of the convergent SJW–CYP3A4 finding lies in the fact that four analytical approaches with different signal sources, different statistical assumptions, and different data subsets independently recover it. In the bibliometrix analysis of the 3,091-record WoS subset (Section 3.1), St. John's wort appears in the niche-frontier Quadrant 2 of the Callon thematic map—a topologically isolated, internally well-connected research cluster typical of mature, citation-recursive paradigms. In the CiteSpace burst analysis of the same subset (Section 3.2), "St. John's wort" produces the strongest historical citation burst in the entire corpus (burst strength 27.01, 2005–2012, eight years), a temporal signature that matches the published case-report wave linking SJW to warfarin and to CYP3A4 induction. In the topic × mechanism Fisher analysis on the integrated 9,413-record corpus (Section 3.4), the HDBSCAN cluster #5 ("antidepressants / antidepressant / wort sjw") shows a strong enrichment for the CYP_induction mechanism category (OR = 26.7, q = 9.5 × 10⁻¹⁵). In the three-tier herbal-taxonomy analysis (Section 3.6), *Hypericum perforatum* is the sole entity in our corpus that achieves a FULL_CHAIN signature, with monotonically increasing odds ratios across resolution layers: Hypericaceae × CYP_induction (OR = 14.20), *H. perforatum* × CYP_induction (OR = 14.71), and hyperforin × CYP_induction (OR = 18.73).

The case is unusually clean for three biological reasons that we expect to be uncommon for other herbal–drug interaction pairs. Hypericaceae is effectively monogeneric in the indexed mechanistic literature, with *H. perforatum* the only widely studied representative, so signal at the family tier cannot be diluted by congeners with different mechanistic profiles. Within the species, hyperforin is the dominant pharmacologically characterised constituent, with reasonably consistent reporting of its CYP3A4-inducing role across the literature. And the underlying biology is mechanistically resolved: SJW activation of the pregnane X receptor (PXR) and consequent CYP3A4 induction is one of the more thoroughly mapped herb-derived nuclear-receptor pathways. We use this case throughout the paper not because it is typical of the field, but because it functions as the natural calibration target against which the other herbal–drug interaction patterns can be compared.

## §4.3 Mechanism-specific natural resolution layers

The three-tier herbal-taxonomy analysis (Section 3.6) yields what we consider the principal methodological contribution of this work. Across the 13 cells that reached significance in the family, species, or compound tier, the resolution scale at which a mechanism produces a statistically detectable signal varies systematically with the mechanism class itself. CYP_induction concentrates at the compound resolution: only one cell in the entire compound tier reached significance, and that cell was hyperforin × CYP_induction. UGT_inhibition concentrates at the species resolution: *Glycyrrhiza uralensis* drives a clear species-tier signal that does not survive at the Fabaceae family tier, where it is diluted by *Astragalus*, *Pueraria*, and *Sophora* species reporting unrelated mechanisms. P-glycoprotein inhibition and broader transporter modulation concentrate at the family resolution: Lamiaceae shows a FAMILY_PERVASIVE signature for both, with no single Lamiaceae species individually carrying the signal but the family-wide aggregation producing statistically robust enrichment.

We interpret this pattern as a substantive claim about the biological scale of action rather than as a methodological artefact of differential field coverage. Enzyme-induction mechanisms typically depend on highly specific ligand–receptor geometry between an inducer compound and the PXR ligand-binding domain, and so resolve cleanly at the compound tier when sufficient data exist. Transporter modulation, in contrast, frequently acts through structural compound classes—polyphenols, terpenes, lignans—that are distributed across a botanical family rather than concentrated in a single species, and so resolves at the family tier. UGT inhibition occupies an intermediate species-scoped scale because UGT-relevant compounds typically co-occur as chemotypes characteristic of specific species rather than as taxonomically wide structural classes.

The per-tier discovery rate (family 6/24 = 25.0%, species 5/32 = 15.6%, compound 1/23 = 4.3%) reflects this scale variation rather than statistical undersampling at the compound tier. Single-compound mechanistic studies generate one record per (paper, compound) pair, while species-level and family-level studies aggregate across compounds and species respectively, producing an intrinsic power gradient (family > species > compound) inherent to the field's publication conventions. Recognising this gradient as a property of the literature rather than a defect of the analysis suggests a resolution-aware framework for future herbal–drug interaction research: different mechanism classes call for different inferential targets, with compound-tier resolution warranted only where the field has produced sufficient single-compound mechanistic data, and family-tier aggregation appropriate where compound-class effects dominate.

## §4.4 Era 4 erosion: network pharmacology dissolves the bifurcation

The PK–PD bifurcation that has held for two decades is, in the most recent era of our corpus (P3, 2020–2026), beginning to dissolve. Three cells were classified EMERGING in the trajectory analysis, and all three appeared in P3: topic #27 (metabolites / pharmacokinetic) × absorption_alteration (P1/P2/P3 = 0/0/10), topic #10 (hormone / testosterone / estrogenic) × signaling_pathway_modulation (0/0/7), and topic #20 (cancer / network pharmacology) × signaling_pathway_modulation (0/1/5). The third cell is the analytically most informative of the three: it is the only instance in the entire dataset where a clinical-application-named topic enriches a signalling-mechanism category. The CiteSpace burst record corroborates the timing of this shift, with "network pharmacology" (burst strength 27.59), "molecular docking" (16.1), "oxidative stress" (10.75), and "gut microbiota" (8.74) all entering active burst status during Era 4 and remaining ongoing through the search cut-off.

We interpret this pattern as a methodological revolution in the field rather than a topical shift. The Era 4 emerging signal does not represent a new bridge between the historically separated PK and PD spaces; instead, it represents an expansion of pharmacodynamic depth in topics that previously did not report mechanistically. Network-pharmacology-driven studies, which assemble compound–target–pathway graphs computationally, generate signalling-mechanism vocabulary as a natural product of their analytical apparatus, and this vocabulary now propagates into clinical-application topics that did not previously contain it. The bifurcation persists, but its boundary is starting to shift on the pharmacodynamic side. The four pharmacokinetic infrastructure entities of the field—CYP450 isoforms, P-glycoprotein, the PXR nuclear receptor, and UGT enzymes—remain present throughout the corpus at high baseline frequency but never appear in any CiteSpace burst, marking them as the structural foundation against which the methodological emergence of Era 4 is now observable.

## §4.5 Bridge clusters and the hepatotoxicity translational interface

Although the PK–PD bifurcation is the dominant structural pattern, three topic clusters mediate cross-talk between the two spaces, and one of them does so with a particularly clear mechanistic basis. Topic #17 (hepatoprotective / hepatotoxicity / liver injury) is strongly enriched for organ_toxicity_modulation (OR = 41.3, q = 9.5 × 10⁻⁴⁶) while simultaneously showing significant signal for CYP_inhibition (27 records), CYP_induction (13), and signaling_pathway_modulation (12) (Section 3.4). This is the only cluster in the corpus where a single topic enriches both a pharmacokinetic and a pharmacodynamic mechanism family at the same time. Topics #33 (general herb–drug interactions) and #2 (antidiabetic) show similarly distributed mechanism profiles but with smaller per-mechanism enrichment magnitudes and lower individual q-values.

We read the hepatotoxicity bridge as biologically obligatory rather than accidental. Drug-induced liver injury is the most common organ-level adverse outcome in the herbal–drug interaction literature, and its molecular pathology necessarily traverses both axes of the bifurcation: pharmacokinetic perturbation in the form of CYP inhibition or induction causing substrate accumulation or depletion, and pharmacodynamic consequence in the form of oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and apoptotic signalling. A research field that studies drug-induced liver injury must therefore generate publications that traverse both spaces, and topic #17 captures exactly that traversal. The bridge is therefore not evidence against the bifurcation but a structural prediction of it.

## §4.6 Methodological consistency: internal validation and four-method triangulation

The validity of the four-method framework rests on three independent forms of internal consistency that emerged from the analyses themselves rather than being designed in advance. The first form is tautological self-alignment within the topic × mechanism Fisher analysis. The most extreme single-cell enrichment in the entire dataset was Topic #24 × UGT_inhibition (OR = 505.1, q = 2.83 × 10⁻⁸²), with comparable alignments for #30 × CYP_inhibition (OR = 17.6, q = 9.4 × 10⁻¹²⁰) and #32 × transporter_modulation (OR = 188.2). These self-alignments demonstrate that the SPECTER2 embedding-based topic model and the LLM-based mechanism extractor independently agree on which abstracts describe which mechanism, despite operating on different signal sources (full title-and-abstract semantic representation versus controlled-vocabulary entity extraction).

The second form of internal consistency is cross-tool agreement between bibliometric methods that share neither algorithmic family nor citation network. The bibliometrix three-fields plot (Section 3.1) and the CiteSpace burst analysis (Section 3.2) independently identified the same set of historically anchored keywords for Era 1 (2005–2012): St. John's wort, ginkgo biloba, milk thistle, and grapefruit juice. Co-occurrence community detection and Kleinberg burst detection converge on the same case-report safety paradigm despite operating on different mathematical objects (co-occurrence graphs versus temporal frequency series).

The third form derives from the Schema v3 herb_in_map curation. By restricting the three-tier herbal-taxonomy analysis (Section 3.6) to the 1,676 records with at least one mapped taxonomic identifier (the in_map = True restriction), we ensured that family-tier, species-tier, and compound-tier comparisons drew from the same record pool, eliminating coverage-driven artefacts from per-tier asymmetries. Any resolution-scale variation observed thus reflects the biological resolution of the mechanism class rather than differential field coverage. Together, these three internal-consistency checks support the multi-method-anchored reporting convention adopted throughout Section 3.

## §4.7 Era transitions: classical decline and the keyword-mechanism lag

Two complementary temporal patterns surfaced in the era-stratified analysis and the CiteSpace burst record. The first is the visible decline of one of the field's foundational paradigms. Topic #6 (warfarin / coumadin) × additive_toxicity exhibited a DECLINING trajectory (P1/P2/P3 = 4/1/0), with the canonical SJW–warfarin and herb–anticoagulant additive-bleeding paradigm steadily disappearing from the indexed literature even though the warfarin topic cluster itself remains one of the larger clusters overall. We read this not as the paradigm becoming wrong but as its reporting convention shifting: case-report-driven event recording is being replaced by mechanism-resolved investigation, and additive-toxicity vocabulary is being supplanted by more specific PK and PD mechanism vocabulary in the same therapeutic context.

The second pattern is a keyword–mechanism lag that appears as the inverse of the first. Topic #15 (gut microbiome / microbiota) and topic #22 (network pharmacology / systems biology), both of which entered active citation bursts during Era 4 (Section 3.2), showed no significant mechanism enrichment in any era of the Fisher analysis. The LLM mechanism extractor classified the bulk of their explicit mechanism content as "unspecified" or "other"—not because these topics lack mechanistic claims, but because structured mechanism reporting has not yet caught up with rapid keyword adoption. This lag is itself a methodological observation: the citation-burst analysis and the topic × mechanism analysis operate at complementary timescales, with keyword-level community attention preceding mechanism-level vocabulary stabilisation by several years. The four-method framework adopted here therefore detects both stages of paradigm emergence: the keyword-attention burst that precedes structured mechanism reporting, and the structured-mechanism enrichment that anchors mature research lines.

## §4.8 Synthesis: clinical implications and future directions

Three findings from this work together carry implications beyond bibliometric description. First, the PK–PD bifurcation indicates that the herbal–drug interaction literature has matured along two parallel research traditions whose mutual exclusion is not anomalous but structurally informative: clinical HDI risk stratification should mirror this axis, separately addressing pharmacokinetic perturbation (CYP3A4 induction by SJW-like inducers, P-glycoprotein modulation by structural classes) from pharmacodynamic interaction (anticancer–herb synergism, anxiolytic receptor-pathway interference) rather than collapsing the two into a single interaction category. Second, the mechanism-specific natural resolution layers suggest that the appropriate inferential target differs by mechanism class: compound-level identification is warranted for CYP induction (specific inducer compounds dominate the signal), species-level chemotypic characterisation for UGT inhibition, and family-level structural-class screening for transporter modulation. A resolution-aware framework should replace the current default of testing all herbal–drug pairs at the same granularity. Third, the four-method triangulation framework introduced here is portable beyond TCM and beyond herbal–drug interactions: any biomedical literature with both a controlled-vocabulary annotation layer and a citation-graph layer can be analysed under the same convergence-anchored reporting convention.

Future extensions of this work fall into three natural directions: expansion of the pipeline to broader natural-product and dietary-supplement corpora; linkage of the bibliometric topic structure to electronic-health-record-derived interaction event series to test whether the literature-derived bifurcation predicts which interaction classes generate clinically observed adverse events; and predictive modelling that uses the topic–mechanism enrichment structure as a prior for new herb–drug pair risk classification. The limitations of the current corpus and analytical framework are discussed in Section 5.

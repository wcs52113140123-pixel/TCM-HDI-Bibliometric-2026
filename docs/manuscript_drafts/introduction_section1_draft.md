## §1 Introduction

Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) is among the most widely used complementary medical traditions worldwide, and concurrent use of TCM preparations alongside conventional pharmacotherapy is common in many regions, particularly in oncology, hepatology, and chronic-disease settings. This widespread co-administration generates non-trivial population-scale exposure to herbal–drug interactions (HDIs), a subset of which produce clinically significant pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic alterations of drug exposure or effect. Cases such as St. John's wort (*Hypericum perforatum*) reducing the systemic exposure of cyclosporine, warfarin, and several oral chemotherapeutics through CYP3A4 induction, or grapefruit-juice constituents inhibiting intestinal CYP3A4 activity, have demonstrated that individual herb–drug pairs can produce magnitude-of-effect changes large enough to compromise therapeutic windows.

The scientific response to these clinical observations has produced a substantial and growing body of literature spanning two decades. Mechanistic *in vitro* and *in vivo* studies have characterised hundreds of herbal constituents against major drug-metabolising enzymes (CYP450 isoforms, UGTs) and transporters (P-glycoprotein, OATP, BCRP), while clinical pharmacokinetic studies have quantified herb-induced changes in the disposition of specific co-administered drugs. In parallel, a clinical case-report literature has continued to document putative HDI events as they surface in practice. The result is a sizeable and heterogeneous body of work whose internal structure—which mechanisms attract sustained attention, which herbs dominate the mechanistic literature, and how research focus has shifted over time—has not been systematically characterised at the corpus level.

Bibliometric and citation-network methods have been applied to adjacent fields with productive results, but existing reviews of the TCM-HDI literature have typically focused either on individual herb–drug pairs (narrative reviews) or on summary-level publication counts (purely bibliometric reports), without integrating bibliometric structure with structured mechanism extraction at the document level. This leaves three questions unaddressed in the existing literature: whether the structural organisation of TCM-HDI research reflects identifiable mechanistic traditions, how that organisation has evolved across the post-2000 publication era, and at which level of botanical resolution—family, species, or compound—different mechanism classes are best identified.

We address these questions through a four-method triangulation framework applied to an integrated 9,717-record corpus assembled from Web of Science, Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed covering the 2005–2026 publication window. The framework combines two classical bibliometric methods (the bibliometrix R package for citation-graph community detection; CiteSpace for Kleinberg-burst temporal analysis) with two structural cross-analyses introduced in this work: a topic × mechanism Fisher enrichment analysis operating on HDBSCAN-derived semantic clusters of (title, abstract) embeddings and LLM-extracted Schema v3 mechanism categories, and a three-tier herbal-taxonomy stratification operating on the same mechanism categories crossed against family-, species-, and compound-level herb identifiers. Convergent findings across methods are reported as multi-method-anchored; cases supported by a single method are flagged for cautious interpretation.

Section 2 details the data and analytical pipeline. Section 3 reports the bibliometric, temporal, and enrichment results, with a multi-method convergence audit in Section 3.3. Section 4 develops the substantive interpretation of these findings, with St. John's wort retained as a running anchor case across both Results and Discussion.

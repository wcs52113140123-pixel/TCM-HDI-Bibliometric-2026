## §1 Introduction

Mining mechanistically structured data from biomedical literature at scale has become increasingly central to pharmacological research as publication volumes outpace expert-curation capacity [Lin et al., 2025]. Large-language-model (LLM)-based information extraction has recently emerged as a scalable complement to traditional natural-language-processing pipelines and manual annotation, enabling rapid construction of knowledge graphs and relation networks from unstructured scientific text [Li et al., 2025]. Recent LLM-augmented frameworks have been applied successfully to disease–gene association mining [Li et al., 2025], drug–drug interaction (DDI) prediction and extraction [Zhao et al., 2024], and pharmacogenomic relation extraction, establishing a new methodological substrate for systematic literature analysis in pharmacological domains. In the herbal–drug interaction (HDI) literature specifically—where multi-component traditional preparations interact with conventional drugs through diverse pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic mechanisms—applications of LLM-based mechanism extraction at corpus scale remain rare, and integration with bibliometric and statistical-enrichment frameworks has, to our knowledge, not been attempted.

Two methodological traditions sit in parallel without dialogue. Bibliometric methods (bibliometrix, CiteSpace, VOSviewer) operate on metadata to provide aggregate citation-graph views of research output, co-authorship structure, and thematic clustering, but they cannot resolve which pharmacological mechanisms dominate within a topical cluster. LLM-based mechanism extraction yields fine-grained per-paper mechanism labels but typically lacks the temporal stratification, topic-cluster context, and false-discovery-rate-corrected enrichment framework that bibliometric and statistical analyses naturally provide. No prior pharmacological literature analysis, to our knowledge, has integrated these two methodological layers at corpus scale, nor has it added a taxonomic-resolution layer to test whether mechanism signals concentrate at the chemical-compound, botanical-species, or botanical-family level. This is the methodological gap that the present study addresses.

Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) is among the most widely used complementary medical traditions worldwide [World Health Organization, 2013], and concurrent use of TCM preparations alongside conventional pharmacotherapy is common in many regions, particularly in oncology, hepatology, and chronic-disease settings. This widespread co-administration generates non-trivial population-scale exposure to herbal–drug interactions (HDIs), a subset of which produce clinically significant pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic alterations of drug exposure or effect. Cases such as St. John's wort (*Hypericum perforatum*) reducing the systemic exposure of cyclosporine [Ruschitzka et al., 2000], warfarin [Yue et al., 2000], and several oral chemotherapeutics through CYP3A4 induction [Mathijssen et al., 2002], or grapefruit-juice constituents inhibiting intestinal CYP3A4 activity [Bailey et al., 1991], have demonstrated that individual herb–drug pairs can produce magnitude-of-effect changes large enough to compromise therapeutic windows.

The scientific response to these clinical observations has produced a substantial and growing body of literature spanning two decades. Mechanistic *in vitro* and *in vivo* studies have characterised hundreds of herbal constituents against major drug-metabolising enzymes (CYP450 isoforms, UGTs) and transporters (P-glycoprotein, OATP, BCRP), while clinical pharmacokinetic studies have quantified herb-induced changes in the disposition of specific co-administered drugs. In parallel, a clinical case-report literature has continued to document putative HDI events as they surface in practice. The result is a sizeable and heterogeneous body of work whose internal structure—which mechanisms attract sustained attention, which herbs dominate the mechanistic literature, and how research focus has shifted over time—has not been systematically characterised at the corpus level.

Bibliometric and citation-network methods have been applied to adjacent ethnopharmacological domains with productive results [Yeung et al., 2018], but existing reviews of the TCM-HDI literature have typically focused either on individual herb–drug pairs (narrative reviews) or on summary-level publication counts (purely bibliometric reports), without integrating bibliometric structure with structured mechanism extraction at the document level. This leaves three questions unaddressed in the existing literature: whether the structural organisation of TCM-HDI research reflects identifiable mechanistic traditions, how that organisation has evolved across the post-2000 publication era, and at which level of botanical resolution—family, species, or compound—different mechanism classes are best identified.

We address these questions through a four-method computational framework applied to an integrated 9,717-record corpus assembled from Web of Science, Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed covering the 2005–2026 publication window. The framework combines two classical bibliometric methods (the bibliometrix R package for citation-graph community detection; CiteSpace for Kleinberg-burst temporal analysis) with two structural cross-analyses introduced in this work: a topic × mechanism Fisher exact enrichment analysis operating on HDBSCAN-derived semantic clusters of (title, abstract) embeddings and LLM-extracted Schema v3 mechanism categories, and a three-tier herbal-taxonomy stratification operating on the same mechanism categories crossed against family-, species-, and compound-level herb identifiers. Convergent findings across methods are reported as multi-method-anchored; cases supported by a single method are flagged for cautious interpretation. The traditional Chinese medicine corpus serves as the application case in this study, but the methodological framework is designed to be portable to any biomedical literature corpus equipped with citation-graph and controlled-vocabulary annotation layers.

Section 2 details the data and analytical pipeline. Section 3 reports the bibliometric, temporal, and enrichment results, with a multi-method convergence audit in Section 3.3. Section 4 develops the substantive interpretation of these findings, with St. John's wort retained as a running anchor case across both Results and Discussion. Section 5 catalogues the principal limitations of the analytical framework, and Section 6 concludes with a synthesis of methodological contributions and future-extension opportunities.

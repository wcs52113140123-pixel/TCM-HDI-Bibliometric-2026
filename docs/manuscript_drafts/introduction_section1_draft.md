## §1 Introduction

Mining mechanistically structured data from biomedical literature at scale has become central to pharmacological research as publication volumes outpace expert-curation capacity [Lin et al., 2025]. Large-language-model (LLM)-based information extraction has recently emerged as a scalable complement to traditional NLP pipelines and manual annotation, enabling rapid construction of knowledge graphs and relation networks from unstructured scientific text [Li et al., 2025]. LLM-augmented frameworks have been applied successfully to disease–gene association mining [Li et al., 2025], drug–drug interaction (DDI) prediction and extraction [Zhao et al., 2024], and pharmacogenomic relation extraction, establishing a new methodological substrate for systematic literature analysis in pharmacological domains. In the herbal–drug interaction (HDI) literature specifically—where multi-component traditional preparations interact with conventional drugs through diverse pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic mechanisms—LLM-based mechanism extraction at corpus scale remains rare, and integration with bibliometric and statistical-enrichment frameworks has, to our knowledge, not been attempted.

Two methodological traditions sit in parallel without dialogue. Bibliometric methods (bibliometrix, CiteSpace, VOSviewer) operate on metadata to provide aggregate citation-graph views of research output, co-authorship structure, and thematic clustering, but they cannot resolve which pharmacological mechanisms dominate within a topical cluster. LLM-based mechanism extraction yields fine-grained per-paper mechanism labels but lacks the temporal stratification, topic-cluster context, and FDR-corrected enrichment framework that bibliometric and statistical analyses naturally provide. No prior pharmacological literature analysis, to our knowledge, has integrated these two methodological layers at corpus scale, nor added a taxonomic-resolution layer to test whether mechanism signals concentrate at chemical-compound, botanical-species, or botanical-family level. This is the methodological gap the present study addresses.

Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) is among the most widely used complementary medical traditions worldwide [World Health Organization, 2013], and concurrent use alongside conventional pharmacotherapy is common in many regions, particularly in oncology, hepatology, and chronic-disease settings. This widespread co-administration generates population-scale exposure to herbal–drug interactions (HDIs), a subset of which produce clinically significant pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic alterations of drug exposure or effect. Landmark cases—St. John's wort (*Hypericum perforatum*) reducing systemic exposure of cyclosporine [Ruschitzka et al., 2000], warfarin [Yue et al., 2000], and several oral chemotherapeutics through CYP3A4 induction [Mathijssen et al., 2002]; grapefruit-juice constituents inhibiting intestinal CYP3A4 activity [Bailey et al., 1991]—demonstrate that individual herb–drug pairs can produce magnitude-of-effect changes large enough to compromise therapeutic windows.

The scientific response to these clinical observations has produced a substantial body of literature spanning two decades. Mechanistic *in vitro* and *in vivo* studies have characterised hundreds of herbal constituents against major drug-metabolising enzymes (CYP450 isoforms, UGTs) and transporters (P-glycoprotein, OATP, BCRP); clinical pharmacokinetic studies have quantified herb-induced changes in the disposition of co-administered drugs; and a clinical case-report literature continues to document putative HDI events as they surface in practice. The result is a sizeable, heterogeneous body of work whose internal structure—which mechanisms attract sustained attention, which herbs dominate the mechanistic literature, and how research focus has shifted over time—has not been systematically characterised at the corpus level.

Bibliometric and citation-network methods have been applied to adjacent ethnopharmacological domains with productive results [Yeung et al., 2018], but existing TCM-HDI reviews have typically focused either on individual herb–drug pairs (narrative reviews) or summary-level publication counts (purely bibliometric reports), without integrating bibliometric structure with document-level mechanism extraction. This leaves three questions unaddressed: whether the structural organisation of TCM-HDI research reflects identifiable mechanistic traditions, how that organisation has evolved across the post-2000 publication era, and at which level of botanical resolution—family, species, or compound—different mechanism classes are best identified.

We address these questions through a four-method computational framework applied to an integrated 9,717-record corpus assembled from Web of Science, Scopus, OpenAlex, and PubMed covering 2005–2026. The framework combines two classical bibliometric methods (the bibliometrix R package for citation-graph community detection; CiteSpace for Kleinberg-burst temporal analysis) with two structural cross-analyses introduced in this work: a topic × mechanism Fisher exact enrichment operating on HDBSCAN-derived SPECTER2 semantic clusters of (title, abstract) embeddings crossed with LLM-extracted Schema v3 mechanism categories; and a three-tier herbal-taxonomy stratification operating on the same mechanism categories crossed against family-, species-, and compound-level herb identifiers. Convergent findings across methods are reported as multi-method-anchored; single-method cases are flagged for cautious interpretation. The TCM corpus serves as the application case, but the methodological framework is designed to be portable to any biomedical literature corpus equipped with citation-graph and controlled-vocabulary annotation layers.

Section 2 details the data and analytical pipeline. Section 3 reports the bibliometric, temporal, and enrichment results, with a multi-method convergence audit in §3.3. Section 4 develops the substantive interpretation of these findings, with St. John's wort retained as the running anchor case across Results and Discussion. Section 5 catalogues the principal limitations of the analytical framework; Section 6 concludes with a synthesis of methodological contributions and future-extension opportunities.

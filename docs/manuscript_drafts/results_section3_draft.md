## §3.0 Lead-in (~160 words)

This section presents bibliometric and analytical findings derived from the 
9,413-record TCM herbal-drug interaction corpus (main 2005-2025) and the 
304-record partial 2026 extension defined in Section 2 (Figure 1). 
Sections 3.1-3.2 characterise the corpus via author and journal productivity 
distributions, a thematic-map partition of the keyword co-occurrence network 
(Section 3.1), and citation-burst detection identifying four historical eras 
of research emphasis (Section 3.2), each computed on the Web of Science-format 
subset (n = 3,091; rationale in Section 2.5). A convergence audit 
(Section 3.3) summarises findings supported by at least three of the four 
analytical methods deployed in this study. The remaining subsections present 
this paper's principal analytical contributions: topic x mechanism static 
Fisher enrichment on the full integrated corpus (Section 3.4), its temporal 
stratification across three disjoint research eras (Section 3.5), and 
three-tier herbal-taxonomy stratification (family, species, compound) of 
mechanism enrichments (Section 3.6). Findings concerning *Hypericum perforatum* 
(St. John's wort) recur across all six subsections as a triangulation 
case-study.

## §3.1 Corpus description

The final analysis corpus comprised 9,413 unique TCM herbal–drug interaction publications spanning 2005–2025, plus a 304-record partial 2026 extension (Figure 1; Methods Section 2.2), drawn from Web of Science Core Collection (n = 3,091; 32.7% of integrated), Scopus (n = 7,181), OpenAlex (n = 2,513), and PubMed (n = 3,867). Cross-database coverage spread across single- and multi-source records: 5,797 records (61.4%) appeared in a single database, 1,567 (16.6%) in two, 949 (10.1%) in three, and 1,125 (11.9%) in all four (Supplementary Table S1). Field coverage was 87.1% for DOI, 97.0% for abstracts of at least 50 characters, and 100.0% for publication year. The Web of Science-format subset (n = 3,091; denoted WoS-3091 hereafter) served as the substrate for all bibliometric-convention analyses requiring WoS-format metadata (Methods Section 2.5).

Mean citation density was nearly identical between the WoS-3091 subset (26.41 citations per document; 81,637 total citations) and the integrated corpus (25.72 citations per document; 242,765 total citations), a 2.7% difference indicating that the four-database integration broadened corpus coverage without introducing systematic citation-density bias.

Annual publication output grew from 261 records in 2005 to 737 in 2025, with two distinguishable regimes (Figure 2): an approximately exponential pre-2015 phase (261 to 501 records, doubling in approximately nine years), followed by post-2015 saturation toward 550–650 records per year. The 25 records initially counted in the main corpus for 2026 reflected upstream metadata misassignment from source databases and were reconciled during Stage 5 cross-file deduplication into the partial 2026 extension (Methods Section 2.2; Figure 1).

Author productivity followed a super-Lotka distribution under both disambiguation schemes evaluated (Figure 3a). The bibliometrix D1 scheme (first-initial plus surname) on WoS-3091 returned a Lotka exponent of α = 2.38 (R² = 0.960); the Python pipeline's D2 scheme (D1 plus institutional affiliation) on the integrated corpus returned α = 2.63 (R² = 0.957). Both exceed the classical Lotka value of α = 2.0, indicating that publication output is concentrated among a small high-productivity subset of authors; 72.6% of unique authors under D1 contributed only a single paper. The 0.25-unit α gap between the two schemes is attributable to disambiguation policy rather than substantive author-productivity divergence: surname–initial collisions under D1 merge same-name authors and inflate high-productivity bins (depressing α), while D2 separates these collisions and recovers a steeper tail. The near-identical R² values across schemes (Δ = 0.003) confirm that the long-tailed shape is robust to disambiguation choice.

Journal scattering followed Bradford's geometric progression (Figure 3b). The WoS-3091 subset's 751 unique source journals partitioned into Bradford zones of 13 (core), 81 (mid), and 657 (peripheral), with geometric-mean multiplier 7.11; the integrated corpus's 2,225 unique journals partitioned into 25/224/1,976 with multiplier 8.89. Both values exceed the classical Bradford multiplier of approximately 5, indicating strong publication concentration in the field. The WoS-3091 Bradford core of 13 journals was methodologically heterogeneous: traditional flagship venues included *Journal of Ethnopharmacology* (n = 264 records), *Frontiers in Pharmacology* (n = 110), *Phytotherapy Research* (n = 78), *Phytomedicine* (n = 77), and *Drug Metabolism and Disposition* (n = 48); three other core journals warrant interpretive caution—*Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine* (n = 96), affected by a substantial post-2019 retraction wave; *Molecules* (n = 71), an MDPI mega-journal whose low selectivity admits chemically-focused work of uneven topical fit with TCM herbal–drug interaction research; and *Latin American Journal of Pharmacy* (n = 51), whose concentrated regional submission pattern distorts core-zone composition relative to substantive field contribution.

A Callon-style thematic map (Figure 4) partitioned 233 KeyWords Plus terms (minimum frequency = 5; top n = 250) into four clusters via rank-based median split on centrality and density. The Q4 "Basic" quadrant (high centrality, low density) held a large in-vitro / metabolism / inhibition cluster (5,489 keyword occurrences across 91 keywords) representing the field's foundational mechanistic vocabulary, and a smaller expression / TCM / cells cluster (2,506 occurrences, 57 keywords) representing emerging molecular-biology framings (gene expression, apoptosis, oxidative stress, multidrug resistance). The Q2 "Niche" quadrant (low centrality, high density) held the canonical safety-case literature centred on P-glycoprotein, *Hypericum perforatum* (St. John's wort), and grapefruit juice (1,553 occurrences, 34 keywords), plus a general herb–drug interaction cluster (2,156 occurrences, 51 keywords). Notably, the Q1 "Motor" quadrant—well-developed themes that bridge to the rest of the network—was empty, indicating that TCM herbal–drug interaction literature lacks a dominant fully-consolidated paradigm: the classical safety-case literature forms a structurally isolated citation community no longer engaged by mainstream new-mechanism research, while the molecular-biology vocabulary is structurally central but thematically underconsolidated, suggesting an incipient but incomplete paradigm shift.

An exploratory three-fields plot connecting top countries, authors, and KeyWords Plus terms (Supplementary Figure S2) shows Chinese institutional networks dominating production around TCM pharmacokinetic vocabulary, US authors more strongly connected to grapefruit, warfarin, and CYP3A4 keywords (reflecting US clinical-pharmacokinetics traditions), and European contributors (Germany, UK, Italy) bridging to in-vitro metabolism keywords (reflecting mechanistic phytochemistry traditions).

## §3.2 Temporal-thematic structure

CiteSpace 6.4.R1 was applied to the WoS-3091 subset (Methods Section 2.5). The resulting keyword co-occurrence network—built from 22 one-year time slices spanning 2005–2026—contained 667 nodes and 3,336 edges (density 0.015), with 99% of nodes (n = 663) in the largest connected component (Figure 7). Network-quality indicators met standard reporting thresholds: modularity Q = 0.3802 (> 0.3 standard for clear community structure) and weighted silhouette S = 0.6935 (> 0.5 standard for reliable clustering, approaching the 0.7 high-quality benchmark).

The burst-detection algorithm identified 220 keywords with statistically significant emergence or decline phases. The two strongest bursts are temporally inverted but quantitatively near-identical: *Hypericum perforatum* (St. John's wort) burst from 2005 to 2012 with strength 27.01—an eight-year burst capturing the foundational case-study era of TCM herbal–drug interaction research—while network pharmacology burst from 2023 to the present with strength 27.59, still ongoing at the time of corpus closure (Figure 5). The eleven-year gap between these maxima, paired with their near-equal strength, marks a 22-year paradigm shift from case-driven safety research to systems-level methodology.

The 220 bursts cluster temporally into four overlapping research eras (Figures 5 and 6). **Era 1 (2005–2012)** was dominated by safety-case studies in a complementary-and-alternative-medicine framing—St. John's wort (27.01), ginkgo biloba (9.72), milk thistle (7.78), grapefruit juice (6.97), herb–drug interactions (13.1), and dietary supplements (6.2). **Era 2 (2010–2018)** transitioned toward mechanistic depth in molecular-biology vocabulary (gene-expression, 7.52; multidrug resistance, 7.94), conjugation chemistry (glucuronidation, 7.07), and analytical methods (tandem mass spectrometry, 10.18). **Era 3 (2017–2022)** centred on in-vivo pharmacokinetics with drug-transporter foci (transporters, 7.03) and animal-model vocabulary (rat plasma, 6.71; mice, 7.28). **Era 4 (2020–2026, ongoing)** is dominated by integrative systems-biology terms—oxidative stress (10.75), inflammation (7.42), natural products (7.3), molecular docking (16.1), network pharmacology (27.59), and gut microbiota (8.74)—all six with end-year 2026 and still in active burst at corpus closure.

Modularity-based community detection produced 11 clusters of at least 10 members; LLR cluster vocabularies grouped these into four thematic regions. A CAM-context cluster (#0; 96 members; "dietary supplement") sits adjacent to a classical drug-metabolising-enzyme region (#2 + #4 + #8; 192 members combined; metabolizing enzyme / HLM / aspirin hydrolysis), a TCM-specific herb and product region (#1 + #3 + #6 + #10; 231 members combined; kaempferia / astragalus / evodia / aidi injection), and a systems-pharmacology region (#5 + #7 + #9; 144 members combined; network pharmacology / IBD model / LPS toxicity)—the latter region carrying the Era 4 vocabulary above. Cluster mean publication years span from 2010 (#4, HLM) to 2017 (#10, aidi injection), a seven-year migration consistent with the Era 1 → Era 4 burst trajectory.

Three of the most-cited TCM herbal–drug-interaction keywords—cytochrome P450 (and CYP3A4 specifically; 227 + 75 occurrences combined), P-glycoprotein (148 + 122 occurrences combined), and pregnane X receptor (81 occurrences)—did not appear in the top-25 burst keyword list, despite ranking among the top 30 most frequent keywords overall. This consistent-but-non-bursting pattern characterises infrastructure vocabulary: terms required to articulate any TCM herbal–drug interaction mechanism, but never themselves the locus of a topical surge. Their non-burst status, paired with the strong bursts of network pharmacology and gut microbiota above, demarcates a clear dual structure—a stable mechanistic foundation persistently engaged by, but distinct from, an actively expanding methodological frontier. The four-era classification recovered here aligns 1:1 with the thematic-map quadrants identified in Section 3.1 (Q2 Niche ↔ Era 1; Q4 Basic ↔ Era 4); this independent cross-method convergence is examined further in Section 3.3.

## §3.3 Multi-method convergence audit

Findings in this study derive from four analytical approaches applied to the same corpus but querying different aspects of its structure: bibliometric convention analysis (Section 3.1), citation-burst detection (Section 3.2), topic × mechanism Fisher enrichment of LLM-extracted mechanism labels (Sections 3.4–3.5), and three-tier herbal-taxonomy stratification (Section 3.6). The four approaches operate on different intermediate representations: keyword–document bipartite graphs for bibliometrix and CiteSpace; topic–mechanism contingency tables for Fisher enrichment; and herb–mechanism record pairs across taxonomic resolution for the three-tier analysis. Findings emerging independently in multiple methods are treated as robust to any single approach's limitations.

## §3.4 Topic x mechanism static enrichment

Following the bibliometric overview of Sections 3.1–3.2, we present this paper's principal analytical contribution: a statistical cross-analysis between literature topics (HDBSCAN clusters of SPECTER2 abstract embeddings) and pharmacological mechanisms (LLM-extracted under a fixed 16-category Schema v3 with confidence ≥ 0.7; Methods Sections 2.3, 2.4, 2.6). Restriction to records assigned to a non-noise topic and at least one extracted mechanism yielded N = 1,738 (record, mechanism) pairs spanning 1,662 unique records and a 37 × 16 contingency matrix. Each of the resulting 592 cells was tested with a two-sided Fisher exact test with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction across all 592 tests. Sixty-three cells (10.6%) reached significance at q < 0.05 (34 enriched, 29 depleted). Strong effects—defined as q < 0.05 with OR > 2 and observed ≥ 5 for enrichments, or OR < 0.5 and expected ≥ 5 for depletions—numbered 30 and 29 respectively (Figure 8; Table 2; Supplementary Tables S7–S8).

The combination of strong enrichments and strong depletions revealed a previously undescribed structural property: the TCM herbal–drug interaction literature is statistically bifurcated into two non-overlapping research spaces (Figure 8). Four topics with mechanism-named KeyBERT labels—#30 (cyp inhibition), #24 (inhibition ugt), #32 (drug transporters), and #9 (pxr cyp)—each strongly enriched their corresponding mechanism (OR = 17.6 to 505.1) while statistically rejecting the major pharmacodynamic mechanisms. The exclusion in topic #30 was particularly extreme: zero observed against 47.4 expected co-occurrences with synergistic_efficacy (q = 4.5 × 10⁻²⁴) and zero against 21.9 expected with absorption_alteration. Conversely, three clinical-application-named topics—#11 (anticancer; n = 730, the largest topic), #23 (antimicrobial), and #28 (medicinal plants / anxiolytic)—each strongly enriched a pharmacodynamic mechanism (receptor_synergism OR = 16.2 to 22.6) while statistically rejecting the major pharmacokinetic mechanisms. The most extreme single depletion in the entire analysis was topic #11 × CYP_inhibition: 5 observed against 99.7 expected (q = 1.9 × 10⁻⁴⁷). We refer to this finding as the PK–PD bifurcation and return to its implications in Section 4.

A subset of enrichments served as internal validation by topic–mechanism semantic self-alignment. The most extreme single-cell enrichment was topic #24 × UGT_inhibition (65 observed, 4.09 expected; OR = 505.1; q = 2.83 × 10⁻⁸²), a 15.9-fold deviation from expectation. Comparable tautological alignments held for topic #30 × CYP_inhibition (q = 9.4 × 10⁻¹²⁰), topic #32 × transporter_modulation (OR = 188.2), and topic #9 × CYP_induction (OR = 27.6). These self-alignments do not represent new biology, but they confirm that the SPECTER2 topic model and the LLM mechanism extractor independently agree on which abstracts describe which mechanism despite operating on different signal sources (semantic embeddings of full title-and-abstract text versus controlled entity extraction).

Three further enrichments mapped onto textbook herbal–drug interaction knowledge, demonstrating that the pipeline recovers established biology when present. Topic #5 (antidepressants / wort sjw)—which captures *Hypericum perforatum* publications—was strongly enriched for CYP_induction (23 observed, 3.62 expected; OR = 26.7; q = 9.5 × 10⁻¹⁵), the canonical SJW–CYP3A4 induction relationship. Topic #9 (pxr cyp) was strongly enriched for CYP_induction (OR = 27.6; q = 1.2 × 10⁻²²), consistent with PXR as the principal nuclear-receptor mediator of CYP3A4 induction. Topic #8 (statins) was enriched for transporter_modulation (OR = 11.0; q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁵), reflecting OATP1B1/3-mediated statin uptake. The SJW finding (topic #5) is the same case anchored by the three other analytical methods deployed in this study (Table 1, Section 3.3).

A small number of topics bridge the PK–PD bifurcation. Topic #17 (hepatoprotective / hepatotoxicity / liver injury) was strongly enriched for organ_toxicity_modulation (56 observed, 6.23 expected; OR = 41.3; q = 9.5 × 10⁻⁴⁶) while simultaneously showing significant enrichment of CYP_inhibition (27 observed), CYP_induction (13), and signaling_pathway_modulation (12). This topology is mechanistically interpretable: drug-induced liver injury intrinsically requires both a pharmacokinetic substrate (CYP inhibition causing substrate accumulation) and a pharmacodynamic consequence (oxidative-stress or apoptotic signalling). Two further clusters—#33 (general herb–drug interactions) and #2 (antidiabetic)—similarly span PK and PD mechanism profiles, though at lower individual q-values and without the same magnitude of any single enrichment.

Finally, three large topics (#25 phytochemistry, n = 156; #38 pharmacies / use herbal, n = 236; #37 herbal supplements, n = 147) exhibited no significant mechanism enrichment after FDR correction. These topics do not represent unmeasured mechanisms; rather, they identify a literature subgenre describing TCM clinical context or product composition without explicit pharmacological interaction claims, defining a meaningful operational boundary for mechanism-bearing analysis. The 30 strong enrichments and 29 strong depletions reported here are static patterns observed across the entire 2005–2025 corpus; Section 3.5 next examines whether these patterns are stable across three disjoint research eras (2005–2013, 2014–2019, 2020–2026) or whether the PK–PD bifurcation and bridge-cluster topology have shifted in time.

## §3.5 Temporal stratification

To assess whether the topic–mechanism enrichments of Section 3.4 are stable across the publication window or reflect transient research fashions, we stratified the 1,738 (record, mechanism) pairs into three disjoint publication eras and reran Fisher exact enrichment per (topic, mechanism, era) cell (Methods Section 2.6). Era boundaries (P1: 2005–2013, n = 517 pairs; P2: 2014–2019, n = 687; P3: 2020–2026, n = 534) produced three approximately balanced subsets aligned with the CiteSpace burst-era structure of Section 3.2. For each of the 40 cells exhibiting significant enrichment or depletion in at least one era, we classified the temporal trajectory using an eight-class typology (STABLE, FADING, EMERGING, DECLINING, RISING, TRANSIENT, BIMODAL, WEAK_NONE; Figure 9; Supplementary Table S10).

Of the 40 trajectory-classifiable cells, 18 (45%) were classified STABLE: significantly enriched or depleted in all three eras. The strongest stable backbone cells anchored the PK–PD bifurcation: topic #30 × CYP_inhibition (P1/P2/P3 = 96/166/76 records), topic #24 × UGT_inhibition (23/36/6), topic #32 × transporter_modulation, and topic #9 × CYP_induction. Their pharmacodynamic counterparts—topic #11 × receptor_synergism, topic #23 × synergistic_efficacy, and topic #28 × receptor_synergism—were equally stable. Critically, the strong depletions of Section 3.4 were not transient: topic #30 contained zero records of synergistic_efficacy, receptor_synergism, or absorption_alteration in every era (0/0/0 in each), and topic #23 (antimicrobial) showed zero CYP_inhibition records in every era. Together with 7 FADING and 4 DECLINING cells, 29 of 40 classifiable cells (72.5%) are anchored in pre-2020 publications. The PK–PD bifurcation is therefore a 22-year structural feature of the corpus rather than a recent fashion.

Despite the stable backbone, three cells were classified EMERGING and all were concentrated in P3 (2020–2026): topic #27 (metabolites / pharmacokinetic) × absorption_alteration (P1/P2/P3 = 0/0/10), topic #10 (hormone / testosterone / estrogenic) × signaling_pathway_modulation (0/0/7), and topic #20 (cancer / network pharmacology) × signaling_pathway_modulation (0/1/5). The third cell is the most informative: it is the first cell in our dataset in which a clinical-application-named topic (cancer) co-enriches a signalling-mechanism category. We interpret this not as a violation of the bifurcation but as its first visible erosion: the rise of network-pharmacology and systems-biology methodology in P3 (Section 3.2 Era 4) is beginning to dissolve the historical separation between PK-named and PD-named topics.

Four cells were classified DECLINING. The clearest case was topic #6 (warfarin / coumadin) × additive_toxicity, with P1/P2/P3 counts of 4/1/0: the canonical warfarin–herb additive-bleeding paradigm—prominent in the SJW–warfarin case literature of the 2000s—has visibly faded from the recent literature. This is consistent with the CiteSpace burst record (Section 3.2), in which "St. John's wort" and "dietary supplements" both completed their bursts before 2013. The clinical case-report paradigm that anchored the field's early publications has been replaced rather than disappeared: the P3 emerging cells point to its successor in network-pharmacology-driven signalling studies.

Two large topics that one might expect to dominate the recent era showed no significant mechanism enrichment in any era: topic #15 (gut microbiome / microbiota) and topic #22 (network pharmacology / systems biology). Although papers are increasingly indexed under these keywords, the LLM mechanism extractor frequently classified their explicit mechanism content as "unspecified" or "other"—a keyword–mechanism lag in which clinical and methodological vocabulary precedes the maturation of structured mechanism reporting. Combined with the static enrichment analysis (Section 3.4), the CiteSpace temporal analysis (Section 3.2), and the bibliometric description (Section 3.1), the temporal stratification yields a four-method triangulation: all four independent analytical frames identify the same backbone, with the SJW–warfarin–CYP3A4 paradigm as the historical anchor (Era 1, Quadrant 2, FADING/DECLINING) and network-pharmacology / signalling-pathway research as the dominant emergent frontier (Era 4, P3, EMERGING). Section 3.6 next examines whether the herbal taxonomy underlying these mechanism patterns resolves at the Linnaean-family, species, or compound level.

## §3.6 Three-tier herbal-taxonomy stratification

The analyses of Sections 3.4 and 3.5 used HDBSCAN topic clusters as the unit of analysis. To examine whether the herbal entities underlying these clusters resolve at a botanical-family, species, or chemical-compound level, we performed a parallel enrichment analysis using the herb-taxonomy fields extracted by Schema v3 (Methods Section 2.4). The 1,676 records with at least one taxonomic identifier were tested against the 16-mechanism schema separately at three tiers—family (24 families meeting n ≥ 10), species (32 species meeting n ≥ 10), and compound (23 compounds meeting n ≥ 5)—yielding 1,241 Fisher exact tests with BH-FDR correction. Thirteen cells were enriched (Family: 6; Species: 5; Compound: 1) and one family-tier cell was depleted. Each multi-tier enrichment chain was classified into an eight-class typology, recovering 15 significantly enriched chains dominated by FAMILY_PERVASIVE (8), SPECIES_SPECIFIC (3), and a single FULL_CHAIN (Figure 10; Supplementary Table S11).

The only FULL_CHAIN—significant enrichment at family, species, and compound tiers for the same mechanism—was *Hypericum perforatum* and CYP_induction (Figure 11). The chain showed monotonic odds-ratio escalation across resolution layers: Hypericaceae × CYP_induction (OR = 14.20; q = 2.9 × 10⁻¹⁷), *H. perforatum* × CYP_induction (OR = 14.71; q = 4.4 × 10⁻¹⁷), and hyperforin × CYP_induction (OR = 18.73; q = 3.9 × 10⁻⁵). The OR increase indicates that within Hypericaceae the CYP_induction signal is concentrated in *H. perforatum* rather than diluted across congeners, and within *H. perforatum* in hyperforin rather than diluted across other constituents. This is the same case anchored by the bibliometric (Section 3.1, Quadrant 2), CiteSpace (Section 3.2, Era 1), static topic (Section 3.4, topic #5), and temporal (Section 3.5, STABLE) analyses—now reproduced as a four-tier hierarchy in the herbal-taxonomy frame.

The eight FAMILY_PERVASIVE chains were dominated by Lamiaceae, enriched for both P-glycoprotein_inhibition and transporter_modulation; the species-tier signal within Lamiaceae did not concentrate in a single member but distributed across multiple species, indicating a family-wide compound-class-mediated mechanism rather than a species-specific effect. The per-tier discovery rate was strongly asymmetric: family 6/24 = 25.0%, species 5/32 = 15.6%, compound 1/23 = 4.3%. The compound-tier sparsity is itself a finding: papers reporting on a herbal species rarely report the implicated compound in a form recoverable by Schema v3.

Three SPECIES_SPECIFIC chains were observed; the clearest was *Glycyrrhiza uralensis* × UGT_inhibition—significant at the species tier but not at the Fabaceae family tier, where the licorice signal is diluted by *Astragalus*, *Pueraria*, and *Sophora* species reporting unrelated mechanisms. Across all 13 significant cells, the resolution scale varied systematically with mechanism class: CYP_induction concentrated at compound resolution (hyperforin); UGT_inhibition concentrated at species resolution (*G. uralensis*); P-glycoprotein and transporter modulation concentrated at family resolution (Lamiaceae). We interpret this as a substantive claim about the biological scale of action—enzyme-induction mechanisms depending on specific inducer compounds, transporter-modulation mechanisms acting through structural classes shared across a botanical family, and UGT-inhibition occupying an intermediate species-scoped scale. The herbal-taxonomy stratification thus complements the mechanism-tier analyses of Sections 3.4 and 3.5 by identifying the natural scale at which each HDI mechanism class is best resolved, a synthesis we develop further in Section 4.

## §5 Limitations

The bibliometric convention analyses (Section 2.5) and the citation-burst detection were applied to a WoS-only subset of the corpus (n = 3,091 of 9,413 main records, 32.7%) because both the bibliometrix R package and CiteSpace require WoS-format input fields that could not be reconstructed with the same standardisation confidence after cross-database harmonisation. Conclusions about author productivity (Lotka's law), journal scattering (Bradford's law), thematic-map quadrant placement, and citation-burst dynamics therefore reflect this subset rather than the full corpus. Comparison of the WoS-subset and integrated-corpus average-citations-per-document (26.41 vs 25.72; 2.7% difference) suggests the subset is broadly representative, but selection effects on specific journal types remain possible and should be considered in interpretation of Section 3.1 and 3.2 results.

Mechanism extraction (Section 2.4) relied on a single model (gpt-4o-mini), a fixed 16-category schema (Schema v3), and a confidence threshold of 0.7 for downstream enrichment analyses. Approximately 23% of returned interactions fell into the catch-all "unspecified" or "other" categories and were excluded from the Fisher tests; this fraction is informative about the maturity of mechanism reporting in particular topics (Section 4.7) but also represents a hard boundary on the analytical method. Re-extraction with larger or more recent models, or with an expanded schema, may shift the boundaries of catch-all assignment and may surface mechanism categories that Schema v3 did not anticipate. The pilot benchmark of n = 200 records, while stratified by year, topic cluster, and journal impact tier, is small relative to the full corpus, and the categorical agreement rates reported in the pilot may not generalise uniformly across all 39 topic clusters. These extraction limitations are bounded by the independent-annotator validation in Supplementary Methods S2: Cohen's kappa was 0.612 overall (47 records after excluding 3 annotator-flagged ambiguous cases) and 0.867 in the high-confidence stratum, supporting the reliability of LLM-extracted mechanism labels under the confidence-stratification used in primary analyses.

The dramatic asymmetry in per-tier discovery rate (family 25.0%, species 15.6%, compound 4.3%; Section 3.6) was interpreted in Section 4.3 as reflecting the natural biological resolution of different mechanism classes, with single-compound studies generating one record per (paper, compound) pair and therefore intrinsically producing weaker per-compound signal than per-species or per-family aggregation. An alternative explanation—that the field has simply not yet generated sufficient single-compound mechanistic data to cross the FDR-corrected detection threshold—cannot be ruled out from the present data. Distinguishing the two interpretations would require either a larger compound-level corpus or external validation against curated pharmacological reference databases such as DrugBank or PharmGKB.

The four databases used (WoS, Scopus, OpenAlex, PubMed) primarily index English-language journals. While OpenAlex provides some reach into regional and Chinese-language sources, the substantial body of TCM-HDI literature published in Chinese-language journals indexed primarily through CNKI or Wanfang databases was not searched directly. Findings in this study therefore characterise the English-language and internationally indexed TCM-HDI literature, which is the subset most accessible to non-Chinese readers but does not constitute a complete picture of TCM-HDI research output. Many Chinese pharmacological institutes publish in English-indexed journals, partially mitigating this limitation, but a systematic comparison with Chinese-only literature would strengthen generalisation of the PK–PD bifurcation finding and the era-stratified trajectory analysis.

The corpus extends through 14 May 2026; records published in 2026 (n = 304) were retained as a partial-year extension, and P3 of the era-stratified analysis (2020–2026) accordingly has uneven year representation in its closing year. The Scheme B disjoint era boundaries (2005–2013 / 2014–2019 / 2020–2026) were chosen to align with the CiteSpace burst-era structure and to balance subsample sizes across eras, not to optimise any particular trajectory-typology outcome. Alternative era definitions—a five-year sliding window, equal-count partitions, or biological-event-anchored boundaries—could yield different trajectory-class assignments at the margin, although the dominant patterns reported in Section 3.5 (the STABLE backbone of 18 cells and the concentration of EMERGING cells in P3) are large enough that they should remain qualitatively robust to such reparameterisations.
